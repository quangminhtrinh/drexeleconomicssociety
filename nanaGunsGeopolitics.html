Guns, Growth or Geopolitics? What the Surge in Global Defense Spending Suggests

  • March 15, 2025

  • Nana Kwasi Adu-Marfo

As tensions rise and events unravel, global powers are compelled to act in their best interest. Defense spending has always been on the grocery list, but now, more than ever, a shift in priorities has moved it higher up. Members of the European Union are set to increase their defense spending significantly over the next two years, while China has boldly pledged to increase its defense budget as well. These radical new steps are a reflection of US-ignited trade tensions and raging wars in Eastern Europe. While China's motives are indistinct, that of Europe's is clear. The EU's increase in spending is expected to have many effects; in large part, it is meant to improve Ukraine's survival after President Trump halted military aid and intelligence sharing. In the long run, however, it is expected to play a role in boosting Europe's 'sluggish' economy.

On the same day, the Department of the Interior issued a directive for a 60-day suspension of "any onshore or offshore renewable energy authorization." Additionally, in a separate executive order, Trump declared a national energy emergency, citing the nation's "inadequate energy supply and infrastructure." While the order emphasized the need for a "reliable, diversified, and affordable supply of energy," it also blamed the previous administration for creating "a precariously inadequate and intermittent energy supply, and an increasingly unreliable grid." These claims echo Trump's long-standing, misleading criticisms of wind energy, which he has falsely labeled as unreliable and dependent on subsidies.

However, these claims have been fact-checked. Electrical grids can manage the variability of wind power, and onshore wind—which encompasses the majority of U.S. wind turbines—is now cost-competitive with or cheaper than natural gas or coal plants. The executive order has significantly impacted the U.S. offshore wind energy sector, putting at risk proposed projects capable of generating up to 32 gigawatts of power—enough to supply electricity to over 12 million homes. States like New York, New Jersey, and Virginia, which have ambitious clean energy goals, are particularly affected. The order has already had immediate effects. Two weeks after it was issued, New Jersey halted the Atlantic Shores offshore wind project, stating "uncertainty driven by federal actions and permitting" and Shell's withdrawal from the project.

Henrik Andersen, CEO of Vestas Wind Energy Systems, noted that the U.S. offshore wind industry has “come to a stop, more or less with immediate effect” following Trump's order. This setback comes after years of growth in wind energy, which now accounts for about 11% of total U.S. power generation and recently surpassed coal-fired power in output.

Overall, Trump's executive order has created significant uncertainty for the offshore wind industry, threatening clean energy goals, job creation, and energy security in several states. The long-term impact could delay the transition to a fossil-free energy future and increase reliance on natural gas, complicating efforts to combat climate change.
